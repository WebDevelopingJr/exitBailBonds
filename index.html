What You Can Expect At First Appearance ▶
(Do not confuse a defendant’s first appearance with an arraignment. In most cases, a defendant will plead not guilty until the arraignment.) A defendant’s bond status can change in several ways depending on the court’s decision. If someone is arrested under a “no bond” status, a monetary bond amount may later be set. Likewise, a previously established monetary bond can be revoked, placing the defendant back under “no bond” status. The court also has the authority to raise, lower, or maintain the amount of bond. In some cases, instead of posting bond, a defendant may be placed on supervised release, which requires regular reporting to Pretrial staff and, at times, compliance with special court-ordered conditions such as random urinalysis or drug and alcohol evaluations. Additionally, defendants who have strong community ties and are not considered a threat to public safety or a flight risk may be released on their own recognizance, allowing them to return to court without any monetary obligation.

Consideration for Obtaining Bail ▶
When determining the bond amount, a judge considers several important factors, including the severity of the crime, the likelihood that the suspect may flee, whether the suspect’s release could pose harm to others, and the suspect’s criminal history.
